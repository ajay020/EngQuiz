Punctuation

Punctuation helps make your writing clear and easy to understand.

1. Period (.) - Full Stop:
Use a period at the end of a sentence to show it is complete.

Example: I like ice cream. It's my favorite dessert.

2. Question Mark (?) - Asking Questions:
Put a question mark at the end of a sentence that asks a question.

Example: Did you finish your homework?

3. Exclamation Point (!) - Strong Emotions:
Use an exclamation point to show strong emotions or excitement.

Example: Wow! That was amazing!

4. Comma (,) - Separating Ideas:
Use commas to separate items in a list or to set off parts of a sentence.

Example: I need eggs, milk, and bread from the store.

5. Apostrophe (') - Showing Possession or Contractions:
Use an apostrophe to show possession (someone's something) or in contractions (can't, don't).

Example: The cat's tail is fluffy. I can't wait to see you.

6. Quotation Marks (") - Quoting Speech or Text:
Use quotation marks to show someone's exact words.

Example: She said, "I'll be there at 3 o'clock."

7. Colon (:) - Introducing Lists or Explanations:
Use a colon before a list or to introduce an explanation or example.

Example: I have three favorite colors: blue, green, and yellow.

8. Semicolon (;) - Connecting Related Ideas:
Use a semicolon to connect closely related ideas in a sentence.

Example: I love hiking; it makes me feel peaceful.

9. Dash (—) - Emphasizing or Adding Information:
Use a dash to emphasize or add information within a sentence.

Example: My sister— the one with the curly hair— is coming over.

10. Parentheses () - Adding Extra Information:
Use parentheses to include additional information that is not essential to the main point.

Example: The concert (which lasted two hours) was fantastic.

Note:
  • Always end a sentence with a period, question mark, or exclamation point.
  • Commas help separate ideas, while apostrophes show possession or create contractions.
  • Quotation marks are like "speech bubbles" for what someone said.
