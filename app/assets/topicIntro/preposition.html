Prepositions

Prepositions are words that show the relationship between a noun or pronoun and another word in a sentence. They can be used to indicate location, direction, time, manner, cause, or purpose.

Here are some examples of prepositions:

  • Location: in, on, at, near, behind, in front of, above, below, next to
  • Direction: to, from, towards, away from, through, across, into, out of
  • Time: at, on, in, before, after, during, since, until
  • Manner: by, with, according to, in spite of, despite, like
  • Cause: because of, due to, owing to, on account of
  • Purpose: for, in order to, so as to

Here are some examples of prepositional phrases:

  • in the house
  • to the store
  • at 10:00 AM
  • by myself
  • because of staying up late
  • in order to get good grades

How to Use "In" and "Into"

The prepositions "in" and "into" are both used to indicate location, but they have different meanings and usages.

**In** is used to indicate that something is enclosed or contained within something else. For example:

  • The cat is in the box.
  • The water is in the glass.
  • The money is in the bank.

**Into** is used to indicate that something is moving towards or entering something else. For example:

  • The cat jumped into the box.
  • The water poured into the glass.
  • The man walked into the bank.

Here is a table summarizing the difference between "in" and "into":

Preposition Meaning Example
In Enclosed or contained within The cat is in the box.
Into Moving towards or entering The cat jumped into the box.

How to Use the Prepositions "Since" and "For"

Since is used to indicate a starting point in time that continues to the present. For example:

  • I have lived in this house since 2010.

For is used to indicate a period of time that has passed. For example:

  • I have lived in this house for 13 years.
Preposition Meaning Example
Since Starting point in time that continues to the present I have lived in this house since 2010.
For Period of time that has passed I have lived in this house for 13 years.

How to Use the Prepositions "On," "Upon," and "Over"

The prepositions "on," "upon," and "over" can all be used to indicate location, but they have different meanings and usages.

**On**
    • In contact with or supported by the surface of something.
    • At or to a particular place or point.

    Examples:

    • The cat is sitting on the mat.
    • The book is on the table.
**Upon**
    • On the surface of something.
    • Immediately after something.
    • In reliance on or as a result of something.

    Examples:

    • The treaty was signed upon the conclusion of the war.
    • I acted upon your instructions.
**Over**
    • Above or on top of something.
    • Across or to the other side of something.
    • More than a certain amount or number.

    Examples:

    • The meeting is over.
    • The price of gas is over $3 a gallon.
